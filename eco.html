KEY DIFFERENCES

One key difference is how Singapore supports startups through structured ecosystems rather than direct operational involvement. In China, organisations such as Transfar tend to take a more hands-on approach in guiding and supporting startup development. In contrast, Singapore provides a structured ecosystem where startups are expected to execute independently. This ecosystem includes startup hubs such as JTC Corporation's LaunchPad @ one-north, which hosts thousands of startups alongside incubators and venture capital firms, as well as programmes backed by Enterprise Singapore and EDB that connect startups with funding, partners, and market opportunities. Singapore generally provides infrastructure, networking opportunities, access to grants, and mentorship programmes, but support remains facilitative rather than deeply operational. Startups are expected to take ownership of their own commercialisation and growth.


Another difference lies in the role of industrial parks. In China, industrial parks can play a more active role in supporting businesses, including investment, incubation, and scaling assistance. In Singapore, industrial parks function primarily as high-quality innovation environments and clusters. Developments such as one-north, Jurong Innovation District, and Punggol Digital District are designed to co-locate companies, research institutions, and talent, encouraging collaboration, research and development, and testbedding. While these clusters provide strong infrastructure and connectivity, companies typically operate independently within them rather than being directly managed by the park.


Singapore's approach to funding and support is also more structured and risk-calibrated. In China, there is generally a stronger willingness to invest aggressively in scaling and experimentation, supported by both public and private capital. Some China-based programmes take on higher levels of commercialisation risk and provide more intensive support during growth phases. In contrast, Singapore emphasises structured, milestone-based support with accountability. Grants and funding schemes often require co-investment, defined milestones, or reimbursement models, with continued support tied to demonstrated progress. This reflects a broader focus on sustainability, efficient capital use, and long-term viability rather than rapid scaling alone.


Singapore also places strong emphasis on international positioning and trust. While China actively builds international partnerships alongside its domestic priorities, Singapore is already widely recognised as a globally trusted and neutral business hub. Its strong legal framework, robust intellectual property protection, and stable regulatory environment make it attractive to multinational companies and investors. As a result, Singapore does not need to invest as heavily in additional trust-building mechanisms, as it is already seen as a reliable base for regional and global operations.


Finally, Singapore functions more as a regional launchpad than a domestic market for scaling. In China, companies can grow extensively within a large domestic market before expanding internationally. Singapore's domestic market, however, is relatively small, which encourages startups to think regionally and globally from the outset. Companies are typically outward-facing early in their development, leveraging international networks, partnerships, and expansion opportunities across Southeast Asia and beyond. Ecosystem initiatives are designed to support this outward growth and global connectivity.
