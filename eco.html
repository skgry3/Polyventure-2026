KEY DIFFERENCES

  1. Singapore Supports Startups Through Structured Ecosystems Rather Than Direct Operational Involvement

    2. China (Transfer): More hands-on involvement in guiding and supporting startup development.
    Singapore: Provides structured ecosystem support, with startups expected to execute independently.

    Singapore has startup hubs such as:

    • JTC Corporation's LaunchPad @ one-north, a major startup cluster with thousands of startups and numerous incubators/VCs.
    • Programmes backed by Enterprise Singapore and EDB that connect startups with funding, partners, and markets.


    Singapore generally provides:

    • workspace and infrastructure,
    • networking opportunities,
    • access to grants and funding,
    • mentorship and accelerator programmes.


    However, support is typically facilitative rather than deeply operational.
    Startups are expected to drive their own commercialisation and execution, compared to more hands-on models seen in some China-based platforms and programmes.

  2. Singapore's Industrial Parks Are More Infrastructure and Cluster Focused

    3. China: Industrial parks can play an active role in business support, investment, and scaling.
    Singapore: Industrial parks function primarily as high-quality innovation environments and clusters.

    Singapore builds innovation districts like:

    • one-north (Biotech, MedTech, tech startups)
    • Jurong Innovation District
    • Punggol Digital District


    These clusters are designed to co-locate companies, research institutions, and talent to encourage collaboration, R&D, and testbedding.

    While they provide strong infrastructure and connectivity, companies typically operate independently within them, rather than being directly managed by the park.

  3. Singapore Is More Structured and Risk-Calibrated in Support

    4. China: Strong willingness to invest aggressively in scaling and experimentation, supported by public and private capital.
    Singapore: Emphasises structured, milestone-based support with accountability.

    Some China-based programmes may take on higher levels of commercialisation risk and provide more intensive support during scaling.

    In Singapore, grants and funding often require co-investment, defined milestones, or reimbursement structures, with increasing support tied to demonstrated progress.

    This reflects a broader emphasis on sustainability, efficient capital use, and long-term viability rather than rapid scaling alone.

  4. Singapore Strongly Focuses on International Positioning and Trust

    5. China: Actively builds international partnerships while balancing domestic priorities.
    Singapore: Positioned as a globally trusted, neutral business and innovation hub.

    Singapore’s legal system, strong IP protection, and stable regulatory environment make it attractive to multinational companies and investors.

    This reduces the need for additional trust-building mechanisms, as the country is already widely recognised as a reliable base for regional and global operations.

  5. Singapore Acts More as a Regional Launchpad Than a Domestic Giant Market

    6. China: Companies often scale within a large domestic market before expanding globally.
    Singapore: Startups are encouraged to scale regionally and internationally from an early stage.

    China’s large domestic market provides significant room for internal growth and scaling.

    Singapore’s smaller domestic market means startups are typically outward-facing from the beginning, leveraging international networks, partnerships, and regional expansion opportunities.

    Ecosystem initiatives are designed to support this outward expansion into Southeast Asia and beyond.
