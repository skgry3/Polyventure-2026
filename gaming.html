KEY DIFFERENCES

One key difference is the focus on global and regional clients instead of domestic mega platforms. In China, digital marketing is strongly centred around a massive domestic ecosystem, including platforms such as Taobao, Douyin, and Xiaohongshu, although many firms are increasingly expanding internationally. In contrast, Singapore-based companies tend to focus on regional markets across Southeast Asia and the broader Asia-Pacific region, as well as global B2B and B2C clients. Firms such as Construct Digital and Verz Design reflect this outward-facing approach, offering integrated digital marketing and UI/UX services to international clients. This differs from China’s ecosystem, which is largely driven by its large domestic user base and platform-centric commerce environment.


Another major difference lies in the platform ecosystem and data environment. China operates within a platform-centric digital landscape dominated by ecosystems such as WeChat, Alibaba, and Douyin. These platforms integrate social media, payments, and e-commerce, allowing marketers to access rich consumer data and execute highly targeted campaigns. Singapore, on the other hand, operates within an open and more fragmented global internet environment, governed by stricter data protection regulations such as the Personal Data Protection Act (PDPA). As a result, marketing campaigns in Singapore often need to span multiple platforms and rely on comparatively less centralised data, requiring different strategic and creative approaches.


There is also less reliance on KOL and KOC culture in Singapore compared to China. In China, social commerce is highly influencer-driven and deeply embedded within digital platforms, forming a core part of the consumer purchasing journey. While Singapore does make use of influencer marketing, it tends to adopt a more balanced marketing mix. Campaigns are generally less intensive, more brand-driven, and less dependent on fully integrated social commerce ecosystems. As such, influencer-led commerce does not play as dominant a role in Singapore as it does on platforms like Xiaohongshu and Douyin.


Finally, differences can be seen in team structure and work dynamics. In China, companies often operate with larger and more specialised teams to support operations at scale. In contrast, Singapore firms typically maintain leaner teams with more cross-functional roles. Employees are often expected to be multi-skilled and take on broader responsibilities across different functions. This reflects a focus on efficiency and productivity, shaped by Singapore’s smaller talent pool and higher operating costs.
