KEY DIFFERENCES

  1. Focus on Global/Regional Clients Instead of Domestic Mega Platforms

    2. China: Focus on massive domestic market (Taobao, Douyin, Xiaohongshu).
    Singapore: Focus on regional/global business markets.

    Singapore firms usually serve:

    • Southeast Asia/APAC market
    • International B2B/B2C clients


    Example:
    Construct Digital
    Verz Design is commonly recognised for integrated digital marketing/UI/UX.

    This contrasts with China's digital marketing ecosystem, which is built around serving a massive domestic market through platforms like Taobao, Douyin, and Xiaohongshu.

  2. Different Platform Ecosystem and Data Environment

    3. China: Closed super-app ecosystem (WeChat, Alibaba, Douyin) with rich consumer data access.
    Singapore: Open internet environment with stricter data privacy regulation (PDPA).

    China's marketing industry benefits from a closed, integrated super-app ecosystem where deep consumer data is readily accessible and campaigns can be tightly targeted.
    Singapore operates under stricter data privacy laws (the Personal Data Protection Act, or PDPA) and an open internet, which changes what is feasible in digital marketing.
    Campaigns here tend to be built on more limited data access and cross-platform audiences, requiring different creative and strategic approaches.

  3. Less Reliance on KOL/KOC Culture

    4. China: Social commerce heavily influencer-driven.
    Singapore: More traditional/professional marketing mix.

    Singapore uses influencer marketing too, but:

    • less aggressive than China,
    • more corporate/brand professionalism focused,
    • less "viral consumer ecosystem" dependence.


    Social commerce exists but has not reached the same level of integration as platforms like Xiaohongshu or Douyin, where KOL and KOC-driven commerce is deeply embedded in the consumer journey.

  4. Smaller Teams, Broader Roles

    5. China: Larger specialised teams operating at scale.
    Singapore: Leaner teams with cross-functional expectations.

    Singapore companies often expect:

    • multi-skilled employees,
    • leaner teams,
    • broader responsibilities.


    This reflects a focus on efficiency and productivity over sheer scale, a pragmatic approach given Singapore's smaller talent pool and higher operating costs.
