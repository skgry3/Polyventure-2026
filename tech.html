Deep Robotics

KEY DIFFERENCES

  1. Singapore Emphasises Deployment Over Manufacturing

    2. China: Strong in both manufacturing and deployment of robotics at scale.
    Singapore: Focuses more on integrating robotics into infrastructure and services rather than large-scale manufacturing.

    This reflects a strategic response to Singapore's smaller industrial base, limited land, and higher labour costs.

    Singapore uses robotics heavily for:

    • logistics,
    • security,
    • construction,
    • healthcare.

    Singapore often imports robotics systems or collaborates with international firms rather than producing them at scale.
    Companies like ST Engineering demonstrate local capability, but Singapore does not compete with China in mass robotics manufacturing.
    Therefore, Singapore focuses more on adopting and integrating robotics, while China is a global leader in both production and deployment.

  2. Labour Market as a Driver of Automation

    3. China: Automation driven by industrial upgrading, productivity, and rising labour costs.
    Singapore: Automation driven primarily by labour scarcity and high labour costs.

    A key reason Singapore invests heavily in robotics is structural:
    a small population and reliance on foreign labour create pressure to automate.

    This creates strong incentives for businesses to adopt automation, compared to China where historically lower labour costs reduced urgency.

    As Chinese labour costs rise, automation adoption has accelerated, but the underlying drivers and timelines differ.

  3. Strong Smart Nation Integration

    4. Singapore deploys robotics into:

    • MRT inspection and maintenance (limited but growing use),
    • security patrol robots,
    • cleaning robots,
    • delivery robots (pilot and controlled environments).


    Singapore’s robotics strategy is aligned with its Smart Nation initiative,
    which promotes digitalisation and automation across public and private sectors.

    However, deployment is still evolving and not yet ubiquitous across all public infrastructure.

  4. Research Commercialisation Through Universities

    5. China: Strong university research combined with large-scale industrial backing and state-led commercialisation.
    Singapore: Focuses on university-to-startup commercialisation linked to national innovation goals.

    NTU and NUS actively spin off deep-tech startups from research.
    These startups are often supported by government funding and industry partnerships.

    This pipeline helps translate academic research into deployable solutions, though the scale is smaller compared to China’s industrial ecosystem.

Liangzhu Lab

KEY DIFFERENCES

  1. Government-Led National Research Ecosystem

    2. China: Strong state-led direction alongside institutional and company-driven innovation.
    Singapore: Highly coordinated, government-planned innovation ecosystem.

    Singapore's biomedical sector is heavily coordinated through:

    • Agency for Science, Technology and Research (A*STAR)
    • Universities like National University of Singapore (NUS)
    • Nanyang Technological University (NTU)


    A*STAR has seconded scientists to support SMEs and local firms,
    reflecting a coordinated national push for technology transfer.

    While Singapore’s system is highly centralised and policy-driven,
    China also uses significant top-down planning (e.g. national funding programmes and industrial policy),
    but operates at a much larger scale with more regional variation across institutions and provinces.

  2. More Startup Incubation / Global Partnerships

    3. China: Combines state support, large firms, and venture capital to commercialise research.
    Singapore: Relies heavily on incubators, venture ecosystems, and global partnerships.

    Singapore strongly supports biotech startups through incubators like NSG BioLabs.
    For example, Singzyme received biotech support and mentorship through the Amgen x NSG Golden Ticket programme.

    This incubator-led approach helps research translate into commercial ventures through structured support, mentorship, and access to global networks.

    In China, commercialisation pathways are more varied, including university spin-offs, large biotech firms, and state-backed funding, rather than a single “lab → patent → company” model.

  3. Research Collaboration is More International, with Stronger IP Protections

    4. China: Rapidly growing international collaboration, though IP protection concerns still influence some partnerships.
    Singapore: Strong global positioning, supported by robust and well-enforced IP frameworks.

    Singapore frequently partners internationally, e.g. NUS Medicine/NUH/NUHS collaboration with Flagship Pioneering.

    A key enabler is Singapore's strong IP protection environment, which increases confidence for foreign partners in joint research and commercialisation.

    China has strengthened its IP framework significantly in recent years, but historical concerns and enforcement variability can still make some foreign partners more cautious.

  4. Talent Strategy

    5. China: Large and increasingly highly skilled domestic talent pool supports scaling and research depth.
    Singapore: Relies on a mix of local and international talent to remain globally competitive.

    Singapore draws heavily on foreign talent to complement its smaller domestic workforce,
    supported by frameworks like the Employment Pass and Tech.Pass.

    This results in highly international and interdisciplinary teams in biotech and research sectors.

    China, by contrast, benefits from scale, with strong domestic STEM output and increasing efforts to attract overseas-trained researchers back to China.

Shining 3D

KEY DIFFERENCES

    China: Designs and manufactures proprietary 3D scanning hardware, supported by strong industrial ecosystems and state-backed funding.
    Singapore: More focused on solution-provider and system integration models, supported by a mix of private capital and government grants (e.g. EDG).

    Singapore tends to focus more on:

    • applying and integrating high-precision technologies,
    • supporting manufacturing users rather than originating many hardware brands at scale.

    This solution-provider model is shaped by differences in market size, industrial base, and capital structure.

    Examples:

    • Shonan 3D Solutions provides 3D scanning and manufacturing integration services.
    • Shonan Design supports R&D, prototyping, and 3D scanning services.


    China's approach—building and manufacturing at scale—is supported by a large domestic supply chain, mature manufacturing clusters, and significant public and private investment.

    Singapore-based firms, by contrast, typically rely on a combination of private investment and targeted government support such as the Enterprise Development Grant (EDG).

    While Singapore does have some hardware development capabilities, it generally does not compete at the same manufacturing scale as China. As a result, many firms focus on customising, integrating, and deploying existing technologies to meet client-specific needs rather than producing hardware at scale.
