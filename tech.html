Deep Robotics

KEY DIFFERENCES

One key difference is that Singapore emphasises deployment over manufacturing in robotics. In China, the industry is strong in both manufacturing and large-scale deployment, whereas Singapore focuses more on integrating robotics into infrastructure and services rather than producing them at scale. This reflects Singapore's smaller industrial base, limited land, and higher labour costs. As a result, robotics is widely used in sectors such as logistics, security, construction, and healthcare. Singapore often imports robotics systems or collaborates with international firms instead of manufacturing them domestically at scale. While companies like ST Engineering demonstrate local capability, Singapore does not compete with China in mass robotics production, instead prioritising adoption and integration.


Another key difference is that the labour market acts as a major driver of automation in Singapore. In China, automation is driven by industrial upgrading, productivity goals, and rising labour costs. In contrast, Singapore's push toward automation is primarily due to labour scarcity and high labour costs. With a small population and reliance on foreign labour, there is strong structural pressure to automate. This creates significant incentives for businesses to adopt robotics, whereas China historically faced less urgency due to lower labour costs. Although this gap has narrowed as wages rise in China, the underlying motivations and timelines for automation remain different.


Singapore also demonstrates strong integration of robotics within its Smart Nation initiative. Robotics is deployed in areas such as MRT inspection and maintenance, security patrols, cleaning operations, and delivery services, although some of these applications are still in pilot or controlled environments. This alignment with national digitalisation goals ensures that robotics is embedded within public infrastructure and services rather than treated as isolated commercial tools. However, deployment is still evolving and has not yet reached full ubiquity across all sectors.


Finally, there is a difference in how research is commercialised. In China, strong university research is combined with large-scale industrial backing and state-led commercialisation efforts. In Singapore, the focus is on university-to-startup pipelines linked to national innovation goals. Institutions such as NTU and NUS actively spin off deep-tech startups, often supported by government funding and industry partnerships. This system helps translate academic research into practical, deployable solutions, although it operates at a smaller scale compared to China's extensive industrial ecosystem.


Liangzhu Lab

KEY DIFFERENCES

One key difference lies in the structure of the national research ecosystem. In China, innovation is driven by a combination of strong state direction alongside institutional and company-led initiatives. In contrast, Singapore operates a highly coordinated and government-planned innovation ecosystem. The biomedical sector in Singapore is closely organised through agencies such as the Agency for Science, Technology and Research (A*STAR), as well as universities like the National University of Singapore (NUS) and Nanyang Technological University (NTU). A*STAR has seconded scientists to support SMEs and local firms, reflecting a coordinated national push for technology transfer. While Singapore's system is highly centralised and policy-driven, China also employs significant top-down planning through national funding programmes and industrial policies, but operates at a much larger scale with greater regional variation across institutions and provinces.


Another difference is in startup incubation and global partnerships. In China, research commercialisation is supported through a combination of state backing, large firms, and venture capital. Singapore, on the other hand, relies heavily on incubators, venture ecosystems, and international partnerships. For instance, biotech startups in Singapore are supported by incubators such as NSG BioLabs, with programmes like the Amgen x NSG Golden Ticket providing funding, mentorship, and access to global networks. This incubator-led approach helps research translate into commercial ventures through structured support. In China, commercialisation pathways are more diverse, including university spin-offs, large biotech firms, and state-backed funding, rather than following a single linear model.


Research collaboration also differs, particularly in terms of international engagement and intellectual property protection. China has rapidly expanded its international collaborations, although intellectual property concerns can still influence some partnerships. Singapore, by contrast, has strong global positioning supported by robust and well-enforced IP frameworks. It frequently collaborates with international partners, such as partnerships involving NUS Medicine, NUH, and NUHS with global organisations like Flagship Pioneering. A key enabler of this is Singapore's strong IP protection environment, which increases confidence for foreign partners in joint research and commercialisation. Although China has significantly strengthened its IP framework in recent years, historical concerns and variations in enforcement can still make some foreign collaborators more cautious.


Finally, there are differences in talent strategy. China benefits from a large and increasingly highly skilled domestic talent pool, which supports both scaling and research depth. Singapore, in contrast, relies on a mix of local and international talent to remain globally competitive. It draws heavily on foreign professionals to complement its smaller domestic workforce, supported by frameworks such as the Employment Pass and Tech.Pass. This results in highly international and interdisciplinary teams within its biotech and research sectors. China, meanwhile, continues to strengthen its domestic talent base while also attracting overseas-trained researchers back to the country.


Shining 3D

KEY DIFFERENCES

One key difference is that China focuses on designing and manufacturing proprietary 3D scanning hardware, supported by strong industrial ecosystems, mature supply chains, and state-backed as well as private investment. In contrast, Singapore is more focused on solution-provider and system integration models, supported by a combination of private capital and government grants such as the Enterprise Development Grant (EDG). This reflects a different positioning in the value chain, where China plays a stronger role in hardware production while Singapore focuses more on application and integration.


In Singapore, companies tend to focus more on applying and integrating high-precision technologies rather than developing large-scale hardware brands. They also often prioritise supporting manufacturing users through customised solutions instead of originating and scaling hardware production domestically. This solution-provider model is shaped by differences in market size, industrial base, and capital structure.


Examples of this approach include firms such as Shonan 3D Solutions, which provides 3D scanning and manufacturing integration services, and Shonan Design, which supports R&D, prototyping, and 3D scanning services. These companies reflect Singapore's emphasis on applied engineering and integration rather than mass production of hardware systems.


China's approach to 3D scanning and related technologies is driven by large-scale manufacturing capabilities, supported by extensive domestic supply chains, mature industrial clusters, and significant public and private investment. In comparison, Singapore-based firms typically rely on targeted government support and private investment, with programmes such as the Enterprise Development Grant (EDG) helping to support innovation and commercialisation.


While Singapore does have some hardware development capability, it generally does not operate at the same manufacturing scale as China. As a result, many firms in Singapore focus on customising, integrating, and deploying existing technologies to meet specific client needs rather than producing hardware at scale.
