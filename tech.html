Deep Robotics

KEY DIFFERENCES

  1. Singapore Emphasises Deployment Over Manufacturing

    2. China: Manufactures robotics products.
    Singapore: Integrates robotics into infrastructure/services.

    This reflects a deliberate strategic response to Singapore's labour market and costs, creating strong incentives to automate.

    Singapore uses robotics heavily for:

    • logistics,
    • security,
    • construction,
    • healthcare.

    Often imports/partners rather than builds giant robotics manufacturers.
    Companies like ST Engineering are indicative of this integration-led approach.
    Therefore, Singapore focuses more on adopting/integrating robotics than manufacturing mass robotics itself while China does both.

  2. Labour Market as a Driver of Automation

    3. China: Automation driven by scale and industrial ambition.
    Singapore: Automation driven by labour scarcity and cost efficiency.

    A key reason Singapore has invested so aggressively in robotics deployment is structural:
    the labour market is tight and labour costs are high.

    This creates a strong commercial incentive to automate, quite different from China where historically cheaper labour reduced the urgency.

    As Chinese labour costs have risen this gap has narrowed, but the starting point and the policy logic behind automation differ significantly.

  3. Strong Smart Nation Integration

    4. Singapore deploys robotics into:

    • MRT maintenance,
    • security patrols,
    • cleaning robots,
    • delivery robots.


    Because Singapore's strategy is tied to its Smart Nation initiative,
    giving robotics deployment a strategic coherence that goes beyond individual commercial decisions,
    embedding automation into public infrastructure and services.

  4. Research Commercialisation Through Universities

    5. China: Massive industrial scaling.
    Singapore: University-to-startup commercialisation, tied to national deployment goals.

    NTU/NUS spin off deep-tech firms from research; NTU spin-offs collectively valued highly.
    This university-to-startup pipeline channels academic innovation into deployable solutions,
    often in partnership with government agencies.

Liangzhu Lab

KEY DIFFERENCES

  1. Government-Led National Research Ecosystem

    2. China: More lab/company-driven innovation.
    Singapore: More government-planned and ecosystem-driven.

    Singapore's biomedical sector is heavily coordinated through:

    • Agency for Science, Technology and Research (A*STAR)
    • Universities like National University of Singapore (NUS)
    • Nanyang Technological University (NTU)

    A*STAR has seconded hundreds of scientists to support SMEs and local firms,
    reflecting a coordinatednational push for technology transfer.
    This top-down planning contrasts with China's more lab- and company-driven model,
    where initiatives tend to emerge organically from individual institutions or commercial players.

  2. More Startup Incubation / Global Partnerships

    3. China: Direct lab -> patent -> company pipeline.
    Singapore: Uses incubators/venture ecosystem to help research commercialise.

    Singapore strongly supports biotech startups through incubators like NSG BioLabs.
    For example, Singzyme received biotech support and mentorship through the Amgen x NSG Golden Ticket programme.
    This incubator-led approach helps research find commercial footing through structured support rather than a direct lab-to-patent-to-company pipeline.

  3. Research Collaboration is More International, with Stronger IP Protections

    4. China: Internal domestic ecosystem growth; IP concerns can limit foreign partner engagement.
    Singapore: International collaboration/global positioning, underpinned by strong IP enforcement.

    Singapore frequently partners internationally, e.g. NUS Medicine/NUH/NUHS collaboration with Flagship Pioneering.
    A key enabler of this is Singapore's robust IP protection framework, which gives foreign partners the legal confidence to co-develop and share proprietary research.
    China's domestic ecosystem has historically raised IP concerns that can make foreign partners more cautious about deep collaboration.

  4. Talent Strategy

    5. China: Large domestic talent pool supports fast, high-volume scaling.
    Singapore: Structured foreign talent frameworks enable diverse, internationally oriented teams.

    Singapore draws heavily on foreign talent to fill gaps in its smaller domestic talent pool,
    supported by formal frameworks like the Employment Pass and Tech.Pass.
    This shapes hiring and team composition in biotech firms, which tend to be internationally diverse.
    China, by contrast, draws from a massive domestic talent base, allowing for rapid scaling within a more homogenous workforce.

Shining 3D

KEY DIFFERENCES

    China: Builds/manufactures proprietary scanning technology hardware, backed by state industrial funds.
    Singapore: Solution-provider/system integrator model, funded through private capital and government grants like EDG.

    Singapore tends to focus more on:

    • applying/importing high-precision tech,
    • supporting manufacturing users rather than originating many hardware brands.

    This solution-provider model is shaped in part by differences in capital access and industrial infrastructure.

    Examples:

    • Shonan 3D Solutions provides 3D scanning/manufacturing integration.
    • Shonan Design supports R&D/prototyping/3D scanning since 1996.


    China's approach, building and manufacturing at scale, is supported by massive state-backed industrial funds and cheap manufacturing infrastructure.
    Singapore-based firms, by contrast, typically rely on private capital and government grants such as the Enterprise Development Grant (EDG), which shapes the kind of innovation they pursue.
    Rather than developing proprietary hardware, Singapore firms focus on customising and integrating existing technology for their clients.
